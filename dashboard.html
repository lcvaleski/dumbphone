Turn On Screen Time

Go to Settings → Screen Time → Turn On Screen Time. Tap "This is my child's iPhone". You'll be prompted to set a Screen Time passcode — use one only you know, not your device passcode.

⚠️ Use a random 6-digit code stored in your password manager. Not a birthday. Not your PIN.

Content & Privacy Restrictions

Under Screen Time → Content & Privacy Restrictions, toggle it on. Key settings to lock down:

  • Installing Apps → Don't Allow
  • Deleting Apps → Don't Allow
  • Web Content → Limit Adult Websites
  • Location Services → While Using / Don't Allow Changes
  • Account Changes → Don't Allow

App Limits

Under App Limits → Add Limit, select categories or individual apps. Set a daily time limit and enable "Block at End of Limit" so the app locks — not just shows a warning.

Tip: Set social and entertainment categories to 0 minutes to effectively block them without deleting them.

Downtime Settings

Under Downtime → Turn On Downtime, set a schedule (e.g. 9pm–7am) where only specific apps work. During Downtime, all other apps are locked — this is the closest thing to "no phone at night."

✓ Always Allowed apps (Phone, Messages) still work during Downtime. Everything else is blocked.

Communication Limits

Under Communication Limits, restrict who your child can call, FaceTime, and iMessage. Set it to "Contacts Only" or even more narrowly to specific contacts you approve.

During Downtime, set it to "Specific Contacts" — your family's numbers only.

Family Sharing Setup

Go to Settings → [Your Name] → Family Sharing to manage Screen Time remotely from your own phone. When your child requests more time, it comes to you as a notification — you approve or deny.

✓ Pair with Apple Configurator supervision so Screen Time can't be bypassed by signing out of iCloud or resetting the device.

Why YouTube Is High-Risk

YouTube's recommendation algorithm is designed to keep viewers watching as long as possible. For kids, this means: one innocent video leads to increasingly intense content within minutes. There is no safe "middle ground" setting that solves this.

⚠️ YouTube Kids is safer but not foolproof. The only reliable protection is blocking YouTube entirely for younger children.

Blocking YouTube on iPhone

Three-layer approach:

  • App Limit: Screen Time → App Limits → set YouTube to 0 min, Block at End
  • Website Block: Content Restrictions → Never Allow → add youtube.com
  • Delete the app and disable App Store installs so it can't be reinstalled

All three together mean YouTube is gone from every access point.

YouTube Kids Setup

If your child is under 12 and you want video access, YouTube Kids is the alternative. Install it, then block the main YouTube app. In the YouTube Kids app, set the content level to Preschool or Younger and enable search-off mode.

⚠️ YouTube Kids is not perfect — content can slip through. Check the watch history periodically.

Netflix & Streaming Controls

Netflix, Disney+, and similar apps have built-in parental controls. Set them up at the account level, not just the device:

  • Netflix: Account → Profile & Parental Controls → set maturity rating + PIN
  • Disney+: Profile → Content Rating → set level + lock profile
  • Create a separate child profile so their viewing history is separate from yours

Blocking Streaming via Screen Time

If you want to remove streaming entirely: Screen Time → App Limits → Entertainment → Block All. Or specifically add Netflix, YouTube, Twitch, and TikTok to the blocked list.

Also block these domains under Content Restrictions → Never Allow: twitch.tv, tiktok.com, reddit.com (videos), twitter.com.

✓ The goal isn't zero media — it's intentional media. Streaming on a shared TV you control is very different from a phone in their room at midnight.

Streaming is under control

YouTube and streaming are the single biggest time sink for kids. Blocking or limiting them has an outsized impact.

The Gaming Risk Landscape

Mobile games are engineered for addiction. The risks aren't just time — they're financial (in-app purchases), social (stranger chats built into multiplayer), and psychological (reward loops designed to keep kids playing).

The most common issues: Roblox, Fortnite, and Minecraft all have chat systems. Kids meet strangers there daily.

In-App Purchase Protection

This is the most urgent gaming setting. Go to Screen Time → Content & Privacy Restrictions → iTunes & App Store Purchases:

  • In-app Purchases → Don't Allow
  • Always Require [your password] for purchases
⚠️ Kids have accidentally (and intentionally) spent thousands on in-app purchases. Lock this immediately.

Game Center & Multiplayer Controls

Under Screen Time → Content & Privacy Restrictions → Game Center:

  • Multiplayer Games → Don't Allow (for younger kids)
  • Adding Friends → Don't Allow
  • Screen Recording → Don't Allow
  • Nearby Multiplayer → Don't Allow

This doesn't stop gameplay — it stops strangers from connecting to your child through games.

Screen Time Limits for Gaming

Under App Limits → Games, set a daily category limit. 1 hour on weekdays, 2 on weekends is a common starting point. Enable "Block at End of Limit" so it actually stops.

Set Downtime to prevent gaming after dinner or before homework. Specific games can be individually limited too.

Console vs. Mobile Boundaries

Consoles (Switch, Xbox, PlayStation) have their own parental control systems. Key differences from mobile:

  • Nintendo Switch: Nintendo Switch Parental Controls app — excellent, real-time
  • Xbox: Microsoft Family Safety app — screen time + spending controls
  • PlayStation: Family Management at account.sonyentertainmentnetwork.com
✓ Console gaming in a shared living space is significantly safer than mobile gaming in a bedroom.
✓

Gaming guardrails are set

Purchases locked, chat limited, time controlled. Gaming is one of the safest places to let kids have fun — with these guardrails in place.

Why Social Media Is Different

Social media is fundamentally different from other screen time. It's not passive — it's a social environment where kids are performing, comparing, and seeking validation from peers. The psychological impact compounds over time in ways games and videos don't.

⚠️ The minimum age for most platforms is 13. But age gates are trivially bypassed. Supervision matters more than policy.

Blocking Instagram, TikTok & Snapchat

Three-step removal:

  • Delete the apps from the device
  • Screen Time → App Limits → Social Networking → Block All
  • Content Restrictions → Never Allow: instagram.com, tiktok.com, snapchat.com, x.com

Blocking both the app and the website closes the two main access points.

What Kids Do to Get Around It

The most common workarounds, and how to close them:

  • Friend's phone: No technical fix — conversations are the solution
  • Browser access: Block Safari + Chrome, set Web Content to Allowed Websites Only
  • VPN apps: Block VPN installs via Apple Configurator profile
  • New accounts: App Store disabled = can't install new apps

If You Allow Limited Social Access

If your child is older and you're allowing limited use, set up these safeguards first:

  • Private account only — no public profiles
  • Followers must be approved by you first
  • Screen Time daily limit (30–60 min max)
  • No social access after 9pm via Downtime
  • Know their login credentials

Monitoring vs. Trust

The goal isn't to surveil your child — it's to protect them while they're still developing judgment. Be transparent: tell them you have parental controls. Explain why. The controls aren't punishment; they're a safety net.

✓ Research consistently shows that the strongest protective factor isn't technology — it's a parent who talks openly about it. The controls buy you time to have those conversations.

iMessage & FaceTime Basics

iMessage and FaceTime are on by default and require no app install — they're built into the OS. Most parents block social media but forget that messaging can be just as risky with unknown contacts.

Start here: Settings → Messages → Filter Unknown Senders on. This puts texts from non-contacts in a separate, unnotified folder.

Communication Limits Setup

Under Screen Time → Communication Limits:

  • During Screen Time → Contacts Only (or Everyone if you trust their contact list)
  • During Downtime → Specific Contacts (your family only)
  • Manage [Child]'s Contacts → review who's in there
⚠️ "Contacts Only" only works if you control what's in the Contacts app. Review it.

Unknown Contacts & Stranger Safety

Kids are approached by strangers through games, apps, and iMessage spam. Teach your child these rules:

  • Never respond to unknown numbers
  • Tell a parent if someone they don't know contacts them
  • Don't share their number in games or apps
  • No video calls with anyone you haven't met in person

Group Chats & AirDrop Safety

Group chats are where a lot of peer pressure, bullying, and inappropriate content spreads. No technical fix fully solves this — but you can reduce exposure:

  • AirDrop: Settings → General → AirDrop → Contacts Only (or Receiving Off)
  • Know about the group chats your child is in
  • Establish a rule: "You can leave any group at any time and I'll back you up"

Third-Party Messaging Apps

WhatsApp, Discord, Telegram, and Snapchat are popular messaging apps kids use to get around parent monitoring of iMessage. If you've blocked social media but not these:

  • Block Discord: App Limits → Social Networking + domain block discord.com
  • Block Snapchat: same as above + snapchat.com
  • WhatsApp: consider allowing with a review of contacts
✓ The App Store install block means once these apps are deleted, they can't come back.

How to Talk About Tech Rules

Parental controls without conversation breed resentment. The most effective approach: be honest with your child about why the rules exist, what you're trying to protect them from, and what they need to do to earn more freedom.

Frame it as protection, not punishment. "I want to make sure you have a great childhood" lands better than "because I said so."

Setting Boundaries Without Battles

Start with co-created rules where possible. Kids who help make the rules are more likely to follow them. Some questions to ask together:

  • What time should the phone charge outside your room?
  • Which apps feel like a waste of your time?
  • When do you think you're ready for more access?
✓ You set the non-negotiables. But giving them input on the edges builds buy-in.

What to Say When They Push Back

Common pushback and responses:

  • "Everyone else has it" → "I'm responsible for you, not everyone else. Let's talk about what you'd do with it."
  • "You don't trust me" → "I trust you. I don't trust the app. They design it to be addictive — even adults struggle."
  • "I'll just use a friend's phone" → "You might. That's your choice. My job is still to make good rules for our house."

Building Trust Around Phones

Trust is built incrementally. A good framework:

  • Earn access: More privileges come with demonstrated responsibility
  • Lose access: Hiding things, lying about usage, or bypassing restrictions = reset
  • No secrets policy: You know their passwords, they know you might check
  • Regular check-ins: Monthly conversations about what they're seeing online

When to Seek Outside Help

Signs that tech use has crossed into something more serious:

  • Extreme anger or distress when device is removed
  • Lying or hiding usage repeatedly
  • Sleep disruption, declining grades, withdrawing from family
  • Secretive about who they're talking to

A therapist familiar with adolescent tech use can help. This isn't failure — it's the same as getting a doctor for a physical problem.

The foundation is communication

Technology changes fast. Your relationship with your child is what stays constant. The conversations matter more than the controls.

Ages 8–10: The Basics

At this age, a phone is not necessary. If a child has one, it should be tightly restricted. Recommended setup:

  • Phone and Messages only (no social, no browser)
  • Screen Time Downtime: 8pm–7am
  • App Limits: no games after 1 hour
  • Phone charges in a common area, not the bedroom
  • No front-facing camera apps
✓ If they're upset about restrictions at this age, that's developmentally appropriate — and means the controls are working.

Ages 11–13: The Critical Window

This is the highest-risk period. Social media pressure is intense, platforms are designed for this age, and the brain is uniquely susceptible to social comparison.

  • No social media — full block, no exceptions
  • YouTube: blocked or YouTube Kids only
  • Messaging: contacts only, reviewed contact list
  • Gaming: 1 hr/day max, multiplayer with known friends only
  • Phone stays in kitchen after 8:30pm
⚠️ This is the age where most kids ask for Instagram. The research on social media and this age group is clear — wait.

Ages 14–16: Earned Trust

Start loosening restrictions based on demonstrated responsibility, not age alone. A trust ladder approach:

  • Allow one social platform at a time, with a private account you're connected to
  • Browser access with content filters still on
  • Downtime moves to 10pm instead of 9pm
  • Monthly check-ins replace constant monitoring

The goal: they're making more of their own decisions, and coming to you when things go wrong.

Ages 17+: Transition to Autonomy

At this stage, hard blocks shift to conversations and soft guardrails. They're going to be independent soon — the goal is habits and judgment, not compliance.

  • Remove most content restrictions, keep the relationship open
  • Talk about specific apps and platforms you're still concerned about, and why
  • Focus on sleep hygiene: phone out of room at night is still valuable
  • Help them audit their own usage in Screen Time — make it self-directed
✓ Kids who have had guardrails their whole childhood tend to set their own limits better as adults. The habits stick.
